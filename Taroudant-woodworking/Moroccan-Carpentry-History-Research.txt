# **The Architecture of Memory: A Historical and Technical Analysis of Carpentry and Traditional Craftsmanship in Taroudant and the Souss-Massa Region**

The artisanal heritage of Morocco represents a continuous dialogue between environmental necessity and aesthetic aspiration, spanning over twelve millennia of human history. Within this expansive narrative, the city of Taroudant occupies a singular position as a repository of Saadian military architecture and a center for a distinct school of woodworking that synthesizes Berber (Amazigh) symbolism with Arab-Andalusian geometric precision.1 To understand the history of carpentry in Taroudant is to engage with the broader evolution of Moroccan "artisanat," a sector that today employs approximately 2.7 million people and contributes nearly 7% to the national gross domestic product.4 This report examines the historical trajectory of Moroccan craftsmanship, the specific architectural and woodworking traditions of Taroudant, the technical specifications of regional timber, and the ancestral methodologies preserved by the master craftsmen, or *maalems*, through the traditional apprenticeship system.4

## **The Millennium Heritage: Historical Foundations of Moroccan Craft**

The evolution of Moroccan craftsmanship is rooted in a deep antiquity that predates the arrival of the Islamic dynasties. Archaeological evidence indicates that Neolithic artisans in Morocco were already producing pottery with geometric patterns as early as 10,000 BC, establishing a foundational visual language that would later be translated into woodworking and textile traditions.1 These indigenous Berber communities utilized the raw materials of their immediate environment—wool from the Atlas sheep, clay from riverbeds, and timber from the dense forests of the High Atlas and the Souss Valley—to create functional objects that reflected their cultural identity and environmental adaptation.7

The arrival of the Phoenicians around 1200 BC introduced vibrant new colors and intricate motifs, while the subsequent Roman occupation introduced the potter’s wheel and advanced woodworking techniques that transformed production methods while respecting local aesthetics.1 The Roman era is particularly significant for its use of massive timber for doorways and structural beams, some of which influenced the later monumental architecture of the Maghreb.3

The defining transformation of Moroccan craft occurred in the 7th century with the arrival of Islam. This period introduced the metaphysical concept of *Tawhid* (the unity of God), which manifested architecturally through symmetry, repeated geometric patterns, and the prohibition of representational imagery.1 This spiritual framework turned carpentry into a form of sacred geometry, where the artisan’s work became a reflection of divine order.3

| Historical Era | Key Craft Innovations and Cultural Influences |
| :---- | :---- |
| Neolithic (10,000 BC) | Initial geometric ceramics; foundational Berber weaving.1 |
| Phoenician (1200 BC) | Introduction of complex dyes and eastern Mediterranean motifs.1 |
| Roman Period | Advance of the potter’s wheel; large-scale architectural timber use.1 |
| Early Islamic (7th-8th C) | Introduction of calligraphy and *tastīr* (geometric patterns).1 |
| Marinid Dynasty (13th C) | "Golden Age" of craftsmanship; perfection of tin-glazing and cedar carving.1 |
| Saadian Dynasty (16th C) | Development of Taroudant as a capital; fortification architecture.2 |

The Marinid dynasty in the 13th century is often cited as the "Golden Age" of Moroccan artistic excellence. Under their patronage, craftsmanship reached extraordinary heights, particularly in the production of vivid tin-glazed ceramics and monumental bronze works, such as the 2.5-meter-tall chandelier in Taza’s Great Mosque.1 This period also saw the refinement of cedarwood carving in the madrasas of Fez, where intricate floral motifs and Kufic inscriptions were integrated into ceilings and portals.3

## **Taroudant: The Imperial Stronghold of the Souss Valley**

Taroudant, frequently referred to as "Little Marrakech," serves as the historical heart of the Souss-Massa region.11 Its identity as a center of carpentry and construction is inseparable from its role as a military capital during the 16th-century Saadian dynasty.2 Under the leadership of Mohammed Ash-Sheikh Saadi, Taroudant was transformed into a fortified base designed to protect the lucrative sugar trade and launch attacks against the Portuguese and Spanish armies occupying the coast around Agadir.11

### **The Architecture of Defense: Walls and Gates**

The architectural centerpiece of Taroudant is its pentagon-shaped wall, recognized as the third most robust historical fortification in the world, surpassed only by the Great Wall of China and the Kumbhalgarh Fort in India.2 These ramparts, spanning approximately 8 kilometers, were constructed primarily using the *pisé* (rammed earth) technique, a traditional Berber method where earth mixed with straw and water is compacted between wooden molds to create massive, insulating walls.8

The walls of Taroudant stand 8 to 10 meters high and vary in thickness from 1.5 to 4 meters, featuring over 100 crenels designed for surveillance and defense.11 The city was traditionally accessed through five primary historical gates (*babs*), which served as critical checkpoints for monitoring visitors and collecting taxes from trans-Saharan caravans.11

| Gate Name | Historical and Functional Significance |
| :---- | :---- |
| Bab Zorgan | Named after the local mills (*grinders*) used for sugar cane and grain.11 |
| Bab Targhount | Formerly the "Gate of Conquest"; the primary sortie point for military campaigns.11 |
| Bab Ouled Bounouna | Named for the Andalusian families who sought refuge in Taroudant in the 16th century.11 |
| Bab Lkhmis | The site of the Thursday market (*Khmiss*); features a 78-square-meter yard and a small mosque.11 |
| Bab Selsla | The ceremonial entrance for sultans; provides panoramic views from its terrace.11 |

Beyond its walls, the Taroudant Kasbah functioned as a military fort *par excellence*, housing the sultan’s palace, a military base, a mosque, and a prison that once held Portuguese captives.11 Another notable structure is Dar El Baroud ("The House of Gunpowder"), a late 19th-century palace built upon the foundations of a Saadian weapons storehouse, illustrating the city's enduring military significance.11

## **The Materia Medica of Carpentry: Regional Wood Species**

The carpentry of the Souss-Massa region is defined by the unique botanical resources of the Atlas Mountains and the arid valleys.9 Artisans select wood not only for its structural properties but for its aroma, grain, and cultural symbolism.14

### **Argania Spinosa: The Endemic Totem**

The argan tree is the unofficial totem of the Souss-Massa region, growing exclusively in southwestern Morocco.17 While the tree is globalized through its oil, its wood has been a staple of Roudani carpentry for centuries.18 Argan wood is described as "amazingly indestructible by insects," a property that led to its extensive use in the construction of regional *kasbahs* and communal granaries (*agadir*).8 The wood's high density and durability made it ideal for structural beams, though its slow growth and protected status under UNESCO now restrict its use to essential traditional tasks and small-scale tool crafting.17

### **Thuya (Tetraclinis Articulata): The Rare Burl**

Thuya wood, known locally as *Araar*, is an exotic evergreen native to the Atlas Mountains and the regions surrounding Essaouira and Agadir.14 It is prized for its rich reddish-brown color and the "dark eyes" found in its burl grain, which resembles burr walnut.9

* **Aromatic Qualities:** Thuya possesses a persistent, pine-like scent that has historically been used for religious incense and is currently valued in aromatherapy.9  
* **Technological Application:** Due to its fragility and rarity, Thuya is primarily used for small, high-luxury items such as jewelry boxes, chess sets, and decorative inlays.9  
* **Historical Prestige:** Thuya was the first burled wood selected for use in the dashboards of Rolls-Royce vehicles, cementing its status as a global symbol of luxury.9

### **Cedar and Noble Hardwoods**

Cedarwood (*Cedrus atlantica*), sourced from the High and Middle Atlas, is the most critical material for Moroccan architectural carpentry.3 Its natural resistance to decay and its workability make it the preferred choice for the massive doors of mosques, the intricate lattice of *mashrabiya* screens, and the hand-carved ceilings of *riads*.3

Other woods utilized in the Souss region include:

* **Walnut and Olive:** Valued for their density and rich, dark tones in furniture making and structural inlays.16  
* **Lemon and Lime Wood:** Used extensively in marquetry to create color contrast; lime wood is sometimes "fried" in used cooking oil to achieve a deep black color for decorative strips.15  
* **Poplar:** Often used for the internal skeleton of bronze-clad doors in ecclesiastical settings.25

## **Geometric Metaphysics: The Rules of Tastīr and Design**

The aesthetic soul of Taroudant’s carpentry lies in its commitment to Islamic geometry, a system known as *tastīr*.26 For the *zawwaqa* (artisan painters) and woodcarvers, these patterns are not mere decoration but are governed by the fundamental rules of *Ḥasba* and *Qasma*.26

### **The Mathematical Framework**

The construction of a Moroccan geometric pattern begins with a central point, from which the design radiates outward to infinity.25 The complexity of the pattern is determined by a specific "coefficient" calculated by the *maâlem*.26

* **The Na'ora:** The central "planetary" star that serves as the heart of the composition.26  
* **Star Configurations:** Common patterns include the *Stachri Bel Mtammen* (a 16-pointed star surrounded by 8-pointed stars) and the 32-pointed star used in monumental mosque portals.26  
* **Floral Symbiosis:** Geometric patterns are often complemented by symmetric floral motifs known as *Tawrīq* (leaf patterns), *Tashjīr* (tree-shaped ornaments), and *Arq* (root motifs), reflecting the Islamic ideal of a garden in paradise.26

### **Symbolism of the Roudani Door**

In Taroudant, the doors of the medina serve as cultural texts. Unlike the purely geometric designs of Fez, Roudani doors frequently incorporate Berber motifs that bridge the gap between desert symbolism and urban Islamic art.28

* **Protective Circles:** Used to signify unity and the deflection of evil.28  
* **Crescents and Eagles:** Symbols carved into the wood to represent Saadian power and military strength.28  
* **Narrative Carvings:** Uniquely, some Taroudant woodwork depicts scenes from everyday life, such as harvesting crops or trading goods at the marketplace, reflecting the social interaction of the Souss region.28  
* **Chromatic Beliefs:** The colors applied to the doors are imbued with spiritual meaning: red guards against bad health, white wards off poverty, and green symbolizes fertility.28

## **The Art of Tazouaqt: Painting and Pigmentation**

*Tazouaqt* (or *Zouaq*) is the traditional art of painting on wood, which transforms structural cedar or poplar into vibrant works of art.26 This craft is essential for the completion of architectural elements in imperial cities like Taroudant and Marrakech.26

### **Pigment Sourcing and Chemical Preparation**

The traditional *maâlem* avoids synthetic paints, preferring to prepare pigments from mineral and organic sources found in the Moroccan landscape.26 These pigments are mixed with a binder of "skin glue" and heated on a charcoal brazier (*mejmar*) to ensure proper bonding with the wood fibers.26

| Color | Material Origin and Processing |
| :---- | :---- |
| Indigo Blue | Derived from the *Nila* plant or copper sulphate (*Ḥedida zarqa*).26 |
| Turmeric Yellow | Saffron or turmeric powder mixed with pomegranate leaf.26 |
| Red Ocher | A combination of paprika, poppy powder, and pomegranate peel.26 |
| Black | Charred sheep's wool mixed with skin glue.26 |
| White | Gypsum or a chalky substance similar to kaolin.26 |

### **The Procedural Methodology**

1. **Priming and Printing:** The wood surface is typically primed with a solid red ocher background. The artisan then uses a handmade powder pad and a stencil (drawn on card stock and cut with a gouge) to imprint the traces of the pattern.26  
2. **Triḥ (Primary Painting):** Using brushes made from donkey mane hair—prized for their flexibility and ability to hold pigment—the artisan paints the pattern with a steady hand, often resting their wrist on the palm of the other hand for precision.26  
3. **Tazyīn (Decoration):** Once the primary colors are dry, the artisan uses a thinner brush to draw fine black or white outlines on the edges of the motifs, providing the "definition" and depth characteristic of Moroccan *Zouaq*.26  
4. **Finishing:** To protect the delicate paint, a varnish based on linseed oil is applied using a soaked cloth, which nourishes the wood and enhances the luster of the natural pigments.23

## **Structural Masterpieces: Mashrabiya and Muqarnas**

Moroccan carpentry is as much a science of environmental management as it is an art of decoration. The *mashrabiya* and *muqarnas* are pinnacle examples of this dual function.22

### **Mashrabiya: The Lattice of Light and Air**

The *mashrabiya* consists of complex wooden screens made from thousands of individually turned wood pieces, assembled without the use of glue or metal fasteners.30 These elements were the original form of air conditioning: jars of water placed in the windowsill would cool the air passing through the lattice via evaporation.30

* **Privacy and Sight:** The etymology of the word relates to both "drinking" (*sharaba*) and "seeing" (*sharafa*), reflecting the screen’s role in allowing those inside to see out while remaining hidden from the public gaze.24  
* **Structural Materials:** Primarily crafted from cedar, walnut, or olive wood, these screens are highly resistant to the expansion and contraction caused by the dry climate of the Souss-Massa.22

### **Muqarnas: The Stalactite Vault**

In the Great Mosque of Taroudant and other sacred spaces, the *muqarnas* (stalactite ornamentation) represents the architectural bridge between the earthly and the divine.3 These three-dimensional carvings, often executed in cedarwood, consist of multiple tiers of niche-like elements that break up light and sound, creating an ethereal, weightless effect in mosque domes and palace entrances.3

## **The Maalem Institution: The Social Structure of Craft**

The survival of Taroudant’s carpentry depends on the preservation of the *Maalem* (master craftsman) status and the traditional apprenticeship system.4 This system is a "living museum of creativity," where knowledge is passed down through generations not through written manuals, but through observation, imitation, and physical practice.4

### **The Apprenticeship Continuum**

Apprentices often begin their journey in early adolescence, spending years mastering the "hidden language" of the workshop.4

* **Gradual Progression:** A student starts with the most basic tasks—sifting clay, Preparing pigments, or sanding wood—before moving on to simple carvings and eventually the mathematical construction of *tastīr*.4  
* **The Menkach:** Apprentices must learn the use of specialized tools like the *menkach*, a unique Moroccan hybrid of an axe and a hammer used for the final shaping of wood and tiles.5  
* **Guild Protection:** Historically, artisans were organized into guilds (*f’soukh*) that ensured quality standards and provided social support to their members.7

### **Modern Preservation and Global Markets**

Today, the Moroccan government has recognized the economic and cultural necessity of safeguarding endangered crafts.4

* **The National Handicraft Register:** More than 440,000 artisans are now formally registered, providing them with access to national social security and professional legitimacy.4  
* **Regional Strategy:** In the Souss-Massa region, a development strategy launched in 2011 focuses on the rehabilitation of crafts, providing technical equipment to artisans, and labeling products to protect them from industrial imitations.6  
* **Export Growth:** Sales of handicrafts reached approximately 10 billion dirhams in 2025, with a significant portion of the growth driven by the demand for traditional pottery (36%) and carpets (20%) in markets like the United States and France.4

## **Technical Conservation: Restoring Historic Woodwork**

The restoration of Taroudant’s historic doors and ceilings is a specialized field that requires a deep understanding of ancient materials and environmental stressors.23

### **The Restoration Protocol**

1. **Assessment:** Professionals inspect the wood for structural integrity, rot, and termite damage. In the Souss region, the primary threat is often the extreme thermal expansion that stresses joints.23  
2. **Cleaning and Paint Removal:** Dirt is removed using soft brushes and mild wood soap. Later additions of synthetic paint are stripped using heat guns or gentle chemical agents to reveal the original *Zouaq* or carving.23  
3. **Structural Repair:** Modern nails are avoided. Instead, damaged sections are repaired using mortise and tenon joints made from the same species of wood as the original (e.g., matching old cedar with new cedar).16  
4. **Conservation Finishing:** Natural finishes like beeswax and linseed oil are reapplied every 12 to 18 months. These oils nourish the wood fibers without creating a brittle film, allowing the timber to "breathe" in the fluctuating humidity of the Souss Valley.23

| Maintenance Task | Traditional Material Used | Frequency/Purpose |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Wood Nourishment | Linseed or Tung oil.23 | Every 12-18 months; prevents cracking.23 |
| Surface Protection | Beeswax polish.23 | Enhances grain and provides water resistance.23 |
| Pest Prevention | Neem or Cedar oil.23 | Natural repellent against termites.23 |
| Metal Hardware | Polish and lubricant.23 | Prevents corrosion on brass and iron.23 |

## **Conclusion: The Living Legacy of the Souss**

The carpentry of Taroudant is a profound expression of a culture that refuses to let time erase its identity.7 From the indestructible argan beams that held the grain of centuries past to the delicate Thuya inlays that adorn modern luxury interiors, the woodworking traditions of the Souss-Massa reflect a unique synthesis of Berber resilience and Islamic aesthetic sophistication.3 The city’s imperial walls and carved portals are not merely historical artifacts but are living symbols of a lineage that continues to adapt and thrive.2 By supporting these artisans and preserving their ancestral techniques, Morocco ensures that its "Architecture of Memory" remains a vibrant, breathing component of the global cultural tapestry, maintaining its spirit "thread by thread and clay by clay".4
